Method

A structural result, not a pessimistic one.

The model has four parameters and one assumption, that the marginal output of a generative system inside a source artist's stylistic neighborhood becomes a near-substitute for that artist's catalog. Listener attention then divides across an exponentially growing inventory, per-consumption value falls at the same rate, and the source artist's earnings under the per-consumption regime carry both forces compounded. The Argument

What the layer is actually paying for.

Spotify's four AI principles, opt-in, fair compensation, transparent credit, and artist–fan connection, all rest on one piece of infrastructure that has to physically enforce them at the moment a track is generated, and the team is rightly building toward it. The question I kept returning to sits upstream of that build. Whatever that infrastructure enforces, it is enforcing a per-consumption royalty whose economic basis generative supply itself removes, because once the supply of style-faithful output approaches infinity, the scarce and therefore valuable thing is no longer the output but the verifiable claim that a given track is the licensed one, made under consent, with the source artist paid. I built a model that shows the month at which source-artist compensation collapses under the current logic, and a running prototype of the layer that issues and verifies that claim.

The prototype I have running in this repository is exactly that checkpoint, and at the moment a generation is about to happen it asks three yes-or-no questions and commits the answer to an append-only signed log, so that a downstream auditor can verify what was decided without trusting the operator. The three questions are whether the source material is licensed, whether the source artist's consent policy on record allows this specific use of their work, and whether the resulting payment to the source is recorded in a form that the source artist can re-derive on their own machine. The signed binary output of those three checks, taken together, is what I have started calling, inside the engineering, the stamp.

A stamped track is monetizable in any venue that requires the stamp, because the consent decision, the licensing decision, and the credit decision are each defensible by an Ed25519 signature over a hash-chained log that any rights holder can replay alone, and the operator cannot quietly change history afterwards. An unstamped track, however technically accomplished, is slop, a thing that cannot pass an audit, cannot clear a brand-safety check at any platform that takes the four principles seriously, and cannot be defended in court if a source artist comes forward. The scarce and therefore valuable asset in a saturated-supply world is admission to the stamped tier, not the output, because the output, by hypothesis, has become unlimited.

The green line in the chart above holds, and I want to flag the asymmetry here, because the priced object becomes the scarce right to be an authorized input rather than a unit of output, and that right's value rises in step with the supply that is making the output cheap, which is one defensible economic design for a licensed AI music layer rather than the only one, where the red curve is the robust claim and the green is one plausible shape for what replaces it.

The reason the enforcement engineers are unlikely to surface this on their own, and I want to say this carefully because the work they are doing is the right work, is that they will be measured on whether their checkpoint correctly enforces opt-in and correctly records payment, both of which it does, and the question of whether the per-consumption royalty their checkpoint enforces remains economically meaningful at scale belongs structurally to a different workstream. Sitting alongside that team and putting in front of leadership a piece of paper that shows, with sliders the reader can move themselves, the month at which source-artist compensation collapses under the current logic is what I would expect a markets-oriented hire to be doing in their first quarter, and it is what this page is.

What Would Have to Be True

The assumption this rests on.

The mechanism is load-bearing on downstream monetization coming to require the stamp rather than treating it as one of several optional rails, and the most plausible mechanism by which that requirement arrives is liability, specifically the combination of legal exposure for unlicensed training-data inputs and brand-safety exposure for platforms that knowingly distribute unstamped material at scale. The stamp becomes the floor the moment a major distribution venue is willing to be sued for accepting work without one, and that day arrives. The question for Spotify in particular is whether they would rather be the issuer of that stamp, the layer that signs it and earns the seigniorage from issuing it, or whether they would rather discover after the fact that someone else already is.

The Prototype

Proof the stamp is buildable.

The running enforcement layer in this same repository is the stamp issuer, the registry, the gate, the signed append-only hash-chained log, the settlement attribution, and the rights-holder statement that re-verifies offline, all behind eight HTTP endpoints in under twelve hundred lines of typed Python with a 95 percent test coverage gate on the domain and services layers. One command resets local state, spawns the server, walks the full loop including a live tamper attempt that the verifier localizes back to the exact entry that was mutated, and shuts the server down cleanly. The repo is at github.com/vaderh-building/consent-ledger, and the demo lives behind make demo.
